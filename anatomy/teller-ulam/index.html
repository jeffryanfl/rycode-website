Anatomy key

A Teller–Ulam thermonuclear device, cross-section. The diagram below references these parts by position only.

T = 0 ns

Intact · armed
Device intact. The fission primary (left), the fusion secondary (right), and the radiation case surrounding both. Nothing has happened yet — but the next 300 nanoseconds will destroy the entire structure.
X-rays flood the case. The primary has released a massive pulse of soft X-rays. They reflect off the uranium-lined casing and fill the radiation channel in nanoseconds. The primary's material blast wave has barely moved.
Radiation implosion. The X-ray bath vaporizes the secondary's outer pusher. Vaporized material rockets outward (gold arrows); by Newton's third law, the rest of the secondary is squeezed inward (orange arrows) at enormous pressure. The fuel is visibly compressed.
Fusion ignites. The spark plug has been squeezed to supercriticality and fissioned, dumping heat and neutrons into the now-compressed fuel. Lithium captures neutrons, produces tritium, which fuses with deuterium. The primary's blast wave is still only ~10 mm out — not even across the case.
Too late. Only now — 300 nanoseconds after detonation — has the primary's material blast wave expanded far enough to reach where the secondary used to be. But the secondary already fused 200 nanoseconds ago. The engineering trick worked: photons beat matter to the scene.

The two speeds

Ratio3,000 – 30,000 ×—

A real thermonuclear weapon is on the order of one to two meters long. The X-ray front crosses it in a few nanoseconds; the mechanical blast wave takes hundreds. Fusion ignition takes ~100 ns. The entire second stage runs to completion in the gap.
